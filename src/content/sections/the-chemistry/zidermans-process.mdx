Ziderman's approach asks a different question: what if the ancient dyers
never needed to strip the bromine at all? What if the authentic tekhelet color was
achieved through a property of the MBI molecule itself — one that requires no UV,
no photodebromination, and no destruction of the bromine bond?

The answer is the thermochromic shift: a physical rearrangement of MBI
molecular aggregates inside the fiber that permanently changes the absorbed wavelength
— and therefore the perceived color — from violet to blue, triggered by nothing more
than mild heat in water.

<SubHead text="What thermochromism is — precisely" />

<TermDef word="Thermochromism">
  The ability of a substance to change color in response to temperature —
  without any change in chemical structure. The molecule before and after
  heating is identical: same atoms, same bonds, same molecular formula. What
  changes is the
  <em>physical arrangement</em> of the molecules relative to each other on the
  fiber surface. Different arrangements absorb different wavelengths. Different
  wavelengths mean different colors.
</TermDef>
This is crucial and bears emphasis: the thermochromic shift in MBI is
<b>not a chemical reaction</b>. No bonds are broken. No atoms are added or
removed. The bromine remains covalently attached to the benzene ring throughout.
HPLC analysis of MBI-dyed wool before and after heating confirms this: the
molecule extracted from the wool is chemically identical before and after. Only
the arrangement of its molecular aggregates has changed.
