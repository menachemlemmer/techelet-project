<p class="bt">Ziderman's approach asks a different question: what if the ancient dyers
never needed to strip the bromine at all? What if the authentic tekhelet color was
achieved through a property of the MBI molecule itself — one that requires no UV,
no photodebromination, and no destruction of the bromine bond?</p>

<p class="bt">The answer is the thermochromic shift: a physical rearrangement of MBI
molecular aggregates inside the fiber that permanently changes the absorbed wavelength
— and therefore the perceived color — from violet to blue, triggered by nothing more
than mild heat in water.</p>

<SubHead text="What thermochromism is — precisely" />

<TermDef word="Thermochromism">The ability of a substance to change color in response to temperature —
without any change in chemical structure. The molecule before and after heating is
identical: same atoms, same bonds, same molecular formula. What changes is the
<em>physical arrangement</em> of the molecules relative to each other on the fiber surface.
Different arrangements absorb different wavelengths. Different wavelengths mean different colors.</TermDef>

<p class="bt">This is crucial and bears emphasis: the thermochromic shift in MBI is
<b>not a chemical reaction</b>. No bonds are broken. No atoms are added or removed.
The bromine remains covalently attached to the benzene ring throughout.
HPLC analysis of MBI-dyed wool before and after heating confirms this: the molecule
extracted from the wool is chemically identical before and after. Only the arrangement
of its molecular aggregates has changed.</p>

<SubHead text="Two logically distinct paths to the thermochromic result" />

<div class="two-col">
  <div class="key-box" style="background:#f0f8ff;border:2px solid var(--ind)">
    <div class="key-title" style="color:var(--ind)">Path 1 — Precursor-direct</div>
    <div class="key-body">
      <b>1.</b> Crush young snails immediately<br/>
      <b>2.</b> Apply gland fluid to wool before air oxidation completes<br/>
      <b>3.</b> Oxidation occurs directly inside the fiber — IND/MBI/DBI formed in place<br/>
      <b>4.</b> Young snail selection → MBI-rich composition<br/>
      <b>5.</b> Heat to 60°C in water → thermochromic shift → blue<br/><br/>
      No vat. No bacterial reduction. The precursor oxidizes inside the fiber itself.
      This is a chemically coherent path and may represent the most ancient practice.
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="key-box" style="background:#f5f0ff;border:2px solid var(--mbi)">
    <div class="key-title" style="color:var(--mbi)">Path 2 — Vat without UV</div>
    <div class="key-body">
      <b>1.</b> Extract and oxidize dye from young MBI-rich snails<br/>
      <b>2.</b> Standard alkaline bacterial vat — leuco MBI forms<br/>
      <b>3.</b> Wool immersed — leuco MBI enters fiber<br/>
      <b>4.</b> Exposure to air — MBI oxidizes back in fiber (appears violet)<br/>
      <b>5.</b> Heat to 60°C in water → thermochromic shift → blue<br/><br/>
      Standard vat chemistry, no UV step. Bromine remains intact throughout.
      This is Ziderman's current documented methodology.
    </div>
  </div>
</div>

<Callout type="blue">
<b>In both paths: the bromine is never removed.</b> Whether the precursor oxidizes
directly in the fiber (Path 1) or the leuco form oxidizes inside the fiber (Path 2),
the final compound is MBI with its C–Br bond covalently intact. The bromine shield
is fully preserved. This is the fundamental chemical difference between Ziderman
and Ptil.
</Callout>

<div class="two-col">
  <div style="background:var(--faint);border:1.5px solid var(--border);border-radius:var(--radius);padding:1rem">
    <div style="font-family:var(--ff-disp);font-size:.95rem;font-weight:600;color:var(--accent);margin-bottom:.5rem">Lever 1 — Snail selection</div>
    <div style="font-family:var(--ff-body);font-size:.88rem;color:var(--ink);line-height:1.68">

      age, and season. Selecting for younger snails at the sourcing stage
      increases the MBI content of the dye before any processing begins.
    </div>
