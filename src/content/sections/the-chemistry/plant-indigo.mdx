  The molecule we described in §1 is not hypothetical. It exists in nature,
  produced by two different plants on opposite ends of the ancient world, and it
  is the molecule the Talmud calls <span class="heb-inline">קָלָא אִילָן</span>{" "}
  — the fraudulent substitute for tekhelet. Understanding it precisely is
  essential before we can understand what makes tekhelet different.

<Callout type="blue">
  <b>
    Both woad and indigo produce exactly the same molecule: indigotin
    (C₁₆H₁₀N₂O₂).
  </b>
  The plants contain different colorless precursors — isatan B and indican
  respectively — but both convert to indigotin through enzymatic hydrolysis and
  air oxidation. The chemistry of §1 applies exactly. Same chromophore. Same
  hydrogen bonding. Same vat chemistry. Same vulnerability.
</Callout>

<SubHead text="What plant indigo cannot withstand" />


  Plant indigo is among the most colorfast natural dyes known under normal
  conditions. Mild soap, rain, perspiration — none of these come close to the
  stripping threshold. The dye is physically trapped in its crystalline lattice
  and nothing mild can break it.



  Its vulnerability is well-defined: the same two keys that dye plant indigo can
  strip it. A mild alkaline environment combined with bacterial reduction is
  sufficient — no extreme conditions required. The two locks disengage. The
  lattice dissolves. The color exits the fiber.


<Callout type="red">
  <b>The critical point: indigotin has no additional protection.</b> No
  structural feature in the molecule raises the stripping threshold beyond what
  mild alkalinity and bacterial reduction can achieve. Whatever strips under
  these conditions is chemically no better than kala ilan. Whatever survives
  must have additional structural resistance. In §3 we will see exactly what
  that difference is.
</Callout>
