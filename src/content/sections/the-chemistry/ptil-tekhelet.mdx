<p class="bt">Ptil Tekhelet's reconstruction of the ancient process was discovered by accident.
When Prof. Otto Elsner of Shenkar College worked with murex extract indoors, the result
was purple. A batch exposed to sunlight came out blue. Investigation revealed that
ultraviolet light, acting on the dye under specific conditions, was stripping bromine atoms
from the molecule and converting it to indigotin.</p>

<SubHead text="The process sequence" />

<div class="steps">
  <div class="step-box"><div class="step-title">Extract gland fluid</div><div class="step-sub">IND + MBI + DBI mixture · oxidized · insoluble</div></div>
  <div class="step-arr">→</div>
  <div class="step-box" style="background:#e8f8e8;border-color:var(--green)"><div class="step-title" style="color:var(--green)">Chemical vat reduction</div><div class="step-sub">Sodium dithionite (Na₂S₂O₄) + alkali → leuco forms</div></div>
  <div class="step-arr">→</div>

  <div class="step-box" style="background:#e8f0ff;border-color:var(--ind)"><div class="step-title" style="color:var(--ind)">Leuco-indigotin</div><div class="step-sub">Br removed · colorless · soluble</div></div>

<Callout type="green">
<b>Note on the reduction step:</b> Ptil uses <em>chemical</em> reduction — sodium dithionite
(Na₂S₂O₄), a modern chemical reducing agent — not bacterial fermentation. The two-key
bacterial vat described in §1 is the ancient natural process. Ptil's modern reconstruction
uses a faster, more controllable chemical method that achieves the same result:
converting the insoluble colored dye to its soluble colorless leuco form.
</Callout>

<SubHead text="Why photodebromination requires the leuco form" />

<p class="bt">This is not a technical detail — it is chemically essential, and it connects
directly to the §1 principle about color and photostability.</p>

<DiagramPanel num="Diagram 13" title="Oxidized DBI vs. Leuco DBI — why one is photostable and the other is not" caption="The paradox: the same property that gives the dye its color (the chromophore) also makes it photostable. Strip the color (leuco form) and you remove that protection. Ptil's process exploits this by reducing first, then exposing to UV. Sequence is everything.">
<div class="two-col">
  <div class="key-box" style="background:#f0f8f0;border:2px solid var(--green)">
    <div class="key-title" style="color:var(--green)">Oxidized DBI (colored) — photostable</div>
    <div class="key-body">
      The fully intact C=C chromophore creates an efficient system for handling incoming
      photons. Wavelengths in the orange-red range are absorbed and their energy is
      dissipated as heat — harmlessly, without breaking any bonds. The blue-violet
      wavelengths that create the color are reflected back to the eye before they
      penetrate the molecule at all.<br/><br/>
      Neither absorbed nor reflected photons reach the C–Br bond with sufficient
      energy to break it. The color itself acts as a shield for the bromine.<br/><br/>
      <b>Result: bromine protected. Cannot be debrominated by UV.</b>
    </div>
  </div>
  <div class="key-box" style="background:#fff8e8;border:2px solid var(--gold)">
    <div class="key-title" style="color:#7a6010">Leuco DBI (colorless) — photolabile</div>
    <div class="key-body">
      The C=C has been broken. The extended conjugation that made the molecule an efficient
      photon-handler is disrupted. The molecule is now colorless and transparent —
      it neither absorbs nor reflects the UV range.<br/><br/>
      UV photons pass through the colorless liquid solution intact, reaching the C–Br bond
      directly. The bond breaks. Bromine departs as a bromide ion (Br⁻). This is
      permanent — the C–Br bond is destroyed, not temporarily disrupted.<br/><br/>
      <b>Result: bromine vulnerable. Debromination occurs rapidly.</b>
    </div>
  </div>
</div>
</DiagramPanel>

<SubHead text="What ends up in the fiber" />

<p class="bt">After photodebromination and re-oxidation in the fiber, the molecule locked
in the wool is <b>indigotin</b> — the same molecule as kala ilan. HPLC analysis of
Ptil-dyed wool confirms indigotin as the dominant compound. The bromine is gone.
It departed during photodebromination as bromide ions (Br⁻) dissolved in the vat liquid.
Any trace bromide ions remaining in the wool are not part of the dye molecule —
they are free ions with no covalent bond to the indigoid chromophore and no relevance
to the halachic or chemical question.</p>

<Callout type="violet">
<b>Ptil's argument</b> does not rest on the chemistry of the final product but on the
authenticity of the source and the process. The Torah requires murex trunculus —
and Ptil's process demonstrably uses murex trunculus. The Talmud itself states that
only God can tell the difference between tekhelet and kala ilan by sight alone,
which Ptil takes as confirmation that the final product is visually — and perhaps
chemically — near-identical to plant indigo. On the halachic question this is
sufficient. On the historical reconstruction question, it is this shiur's position
that it is not.
</Callout>
