Ptil Tekhelet's reconstruction of the ancient process was discovered by accident.
When Prof. Otto Elsner of Shenkar College worked with murex extract indoors, the result
was purple. A batch exposed to sunlight came out blue. Investigation revealed that
ultraviolet light, acting on the dye under specific conditions, was stripping bromine atoms
from the molecule and converting it to indigotin.

  <b>Note on the reduction step:</b> Ptil uses <em>chemical</em> reduction —

<SubHead text="What ends up in the fiber" />

After photodebromination and re-oxidation in the fiber, the molecule locked
in the wool is <b>indigotin</b> — the same molecule as kala ilan. HPLC analysis of
Ptil-dyed wool confirms indigotin as the dominant compound. The bromine is gone.
It departed during photodebromination as bromide ions (Br⁻) dissolved in the vat liquid.
Any trace bromide ions remaining in the wool are not part of the dye molecule —
they are free ions with no covalent bond to the indigoid chromophore and no relevance
to the halachic or chemical question.

<Callout type="violet">
  <b>Ptil's argument</b> does not rest on the chemistry of the final product but
  on the authenticity of the source and the process. The Torah requires murex
  trunculus — and Ptil's process demonstrably uses murex trunculus. The Talmud
  itself states that only God can tell the difference between tekhelet and kala
  ilan by sight alone, which Ptil takes as confirmation that the final product
  is visually — and perhaps chemically — near-identical to plant indigo. On the
  halachic question this is sufficient. On the historical reconstruction
  question, it is our position that it is not.
</Callout>
