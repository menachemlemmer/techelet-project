<div class="intro-title">The Chemistry of Tekhelet</div>
<div class="intro-sub">Shiur Materials — Movements 0 · I · III · IV</div>

<p class="bt">In a previous section we explored how light interacts with matter to create what
we perceive as color — and how pigment represents the truest, most intrinsic form of color.
The pigment we used as our example was the deep indigo blue of <span class="heb-inline">סַפִּיר</span>,
which we identified as lapis lazuli. The Talmud relates that the blue-dyed tekhelet wool resembles
the sea, the sea resembles the sky, and the sky resembles the throne of glory —
described as the color of <span class="heb-inline">סַפִּיר</span>. In this section we turn to
the chemistry of that indigo blue in all its forms.</p>

<p class="bt">Our aim is to use chemistry to faithfully reconstruct the ancient tekhelet dyeing
process — and to show that the Talmudic testing methods align with that chemistry to an
extraordinary degree of precision. Along the way we will examine two modern methods
of tekhelet production and evaluate what we believe is the more historically accurate process.</p>

<Callout type="thesis">
<b>Our position on the halachic question:</b> Both modern methods are valid. What the Torah
requires is the color and the source — Hexaplex trunculus, the chilazon of the Talmud.
Both processes satisfy that requirement. Our argument here is historical and chemical —
and possibly preferential.
</Callout>
