Lapis lazuli is a deep blue semi-precious stone mined primarily in Afghanistan, used as a pigment and ornament since antiquity. In Tanach, the stone called <span class="heb-inline">סַפִּיר</span> (sapir) almost certainly refers to lapis lazuli — true sapphire (the mineral corundum) was unknown in the ancient Near East. When the elders of Israel ascended Sinai and saw beneath the divine presence "like the work of a sapphire brick, like the very body of the sky in its purity" (Shemot 24:10), what they saw was the color of lapis.

The blue of lapis lazuli comes from a pigment molecule called lazurite, bound permanently into the stone's crystal structure. This is the most straightforward type of color: a molecule inside a solid material selectively absorbing certain wavelengths and reflecting others.

<TermDef word="Lazurite">
  The blue pigment molecule responsible for the color of lapis lazuli. Its color
  comes from sulfur radical anions — sulfur atoms that have gained extra
  electrons, giving them the ability to absorb visible light from roughly 480nm
  upward, with peak absorption at 610–620nm. The color is intrinsic to the crystal structure and does not depend on
  surrounding conditions. (Bikiaris et al., <em>Microchemical Journal</em> 125, 2016)
</TermDef>
