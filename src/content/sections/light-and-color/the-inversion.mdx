We see here that the chain is not a gradation of shades of color, but gradations of different mechanisms of color altogether. More specifically, each mechanism of color is associated with a different experience and encounter.

We are comparing the blue thread in the garment first to the mechanism of color in the sea, then the sky and finally the throne. These are ascending levels of coloration, each more related to the interior essence than the one before.

The color of the sea is primarily a reflection of the sky, not related to its own character. The sky is the medium through which light scattering occurs, but it is not absorbing or reflecting any of the light directly. Finally, in the blue stone, the light interacts directly with the pigment — part of the spectrum is absorbed, and the remainder reflected.

Each of these is associated with a specific experience of our interaction with divine revelation. At the splitting of the sea, we experienced God as warrior. This was very real to us. We were rescued from the armies of Pharaoh and crossed the sea in a visceral and real-world situation through direct divine intervention. Yet this experience, while visceral and real, touches only the outermost layer of relationship with God. It reveals nothing of the deeper, spiritual essence. To associate God as the warrior who saved us is extremely reductive. At Sinai, the heavens opened up, the Torah — the expression of divine will — was revealed to us. This is a much higher interaction and knowledge of God. But still, the Torah revealed at Sinai represents the divine will as it manifests in this world. Not the essence of God Himself. That is why it is represented as a color that arises from how divine light and energy scatters when it interacts with the world — albeit a higher plane, a heavenly interaction.

Yechezkel's vision of the throne of glory — the highest level of revelation of the divine essence — is represented by a pigmented stone, where light is absorbed and interacts with the interior of the substance itself. However, even the throne of glory is not truly the divine essence itself. For the essence of God is unknowable and unquantifiable. It neither absorbs nor reflects — rather it is the source of all light, energy, matter and existence.

As we have learned, even in pigmented color, we can only see what is not absorbed — what is reflected. Even in the highest and closest divine revelation, when there is some contact with divine essence, our perception is only what is reflected to us and not what is the interior essence. We can only perceive indirectly, only what bounces off and comes back to us.

This is possibly the meaning of the verse when Moses asks to see the divine glory. The response is that the nature of sight is never seeing the true interior essence. Sight is merely a reflection of light. Even the highest level of revelation is not what is actually the interior essence — <span class="heb-inline">פָּנַי לֹא יֵרָאוּ</span>. Panim can mean face, but it can also mean interior — <span class="heb-inline">פְּנִים</span>. <span class="heb-inline">וְרָאִיתָ אֶת אֲחֹרָי</span> — what you see is only what is reflected and left behind.

Rashi, citing the Midrash, writes that a maidservant at the sea saw what the prophets Isaiah and Yechezkel could not see in their prophecy. This seems to indicate a greater level of revelation at the sea than Yechezkel. But in the chain in Menachot, we established that the vision at the sea was two orders lower than that of Yechezkel. The resolution is simple. From a visceral and experiential perspective, the experience at the sea was the most real and vivid revelation. But in terms of truth and essence, it represents the lowest possible divine interaction. These are two statements coming from differing perspectives.

Now we can resolve the contradiction we noticed in Rashi on Shemot 20:2. In the language of the Torah, truth is what is more interior. Thus the closer one is to essence, the more the color represents interaction with the substance. However, the ordinary human experience is to associate truth and reality with what is most visible. Daylight equals reality. The elders were being shown that truth and reality are the inverse of what they previously thought. The spirit is a higher truth and reality than the physical dimension. But they were shown in their own associative language. They saw the darker, blue-pigmented stone as representing the revelation during the exodus. The higher revelation at Sinai was shown as being represented by daylight.

This brings us to the tzitzit. The Torah commands us to place fringes on the corners of our garments — white strings along with a blue tekhelet string. White represents exterior reality: what is most visible, most physical, most real to us. The pigmented blue string represents light that is absorbed and taken in. It alludes to the hidden spiritual interior reality that we do not see directly. But this hidden interior reality is the true essence that animates and permeates the exterior shell with life. The lesson of the tekhelet is to teach us this inversion — as we see in the gradation of mechanisms from sea to sky and from sky to throne. Our mission is to remember the divine spirit that infuses us with life, and to tend to that reality above all.

Now that we have woven these different threads together into a coherent narrative, let us turn our attention to the pigmented blue thread itself.
