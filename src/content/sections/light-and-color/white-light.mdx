Sunlight — what we call white light — is not a single wavelength. It is a mixture of all visible wavelengths simultaneously. Pass it through a glass prism and it separates into the full spectrum: the rainbow was always inside the white, hidden but present.

Now the key question: when white light strikes an object, what happens? The answer is that the molecules inside the object absorb some wavelengths and reflect others.

The color we see is made of the wavelengths the object did not absorb — the light it sent back. We never see what an object holds. We only see what it returns.

<TermDef word="Absorption">When a photon of the right wavelength strikes a molecule, its energy can be absorbed — captured by the molecule's electrons, which jump to a higher energy state. That wavelength of light disappears from the reflected beam. The molecule has taken it in.</TermDef>

<TermDef word="Reflection">Wavelengths that are not absorbed bounce back from the surface. These are the wavelengths that reach our eye. The color we see is entirely composed of reflected light — the wavelengths the object did not want.</TermDef>

<TermDef word="Pigment">A molecule that selectively absorbs specific wavelengths of visible light and reflects others, producing a perceived color. A blue pigment absorbs wavelengths in the yellow, orange, and red range (roughly 560–700nm) and reflects wavelengths in the blue range (roughly 430–490nm). The pigment molecule itself is not blue — it produces blue by subtraction, by removing non-blue light from white light.</TermDef>
