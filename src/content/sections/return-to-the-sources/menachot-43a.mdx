Menachot 43a records two tests for authenticating tekhelet. To understand what these tests are doing, we need to ask a prior question: what would a precise chemical test for chilazon dye actually look like? It would need to probe something that plant indigo does not have. It would need to be reproducible with ancient materials. And it would need to be robust enough that a dyer in the field could rely on it. The Sages designed exactly that — twice.

<SubHead text="Translation and commentary" />

The Baraita opens with a surprising claim: tekhelet has no test. The Gemara immediately pushes back — Rav Yitzchak bar Rav Yehuda had a test. What does the Baraita mean?

The answer: the Baraita is not talking about chemical identity. It is talking about lishma — the requirement that tekhelet be dyed with intent for the purpose of tzitzit. That intent cannot be established by any test. No chemical analysis can tell you what was in the dyer's mind. For that reason, one must purchase tekhelet only from a trusted expert. The tests that follow address a completely separate question: not intent, but identity. Is this dye from the chilazon? Or is it kala ilan?

Test 1 — Rav Yitzchak bar Rav Yehuda: bring alum, fenugreek water, and forty-day fermented urine. Soak the thread from evening until morning. If the color separates from the fabric — <span class="heb-inline">אִיפְּרֵד חֲזוּתַיהּ</span> — invalid. If it does not separate — valid.

Test 2 — Rav Adda: bring a thick barley sourdough starter and bake the thread inside it. If the color changed for the better — <span class="heb-inline">אִישְׁתַּנַּאי לִמְעַלְּיוּתָא</span> — valid. If it changed for the worse — <span class="heb-inline">אִישְׁתַּנַּאי לִגְרִיעוּתָא</span> — invalid.

At this point the Gemara appears to present two independent tests — either one sufficient on its own. Then a case arrives that forces a reconsideration.

A man brought tekhelet to be tested in the time of Rav Achai. Test 1: the color separated — invalid by that test. Test 2: the color changed for the better — valid by that test. The dayan was about to rule it invalid. Rav Achai stopped him: is this sample neither tekhelet nor kala ilan? If the tests are truly independent, this sample is simultaneously valid and invalid — an impossibility. There is only one coherent reading: the two rulings were stated in relation to each other — <span class="heb-inline">שְׁמוּעָתָא אַהֲדָדֵי אִיתְּמַר</span>. Confirmed from Eretz Yisrael.

The tests are sequential. Where Test 1 shows the color held — definitively valid. Where it separated — inconclusive, proceed to Test 2. Improved — valid. Degraded — invalid.

<SubHead text="What the tests are doing" />

Test 1 is a controlled attempt to reverse the dyeing process. The word <span class="heb-inline">אִיפְּרֵד</span> tells us exactly what success looks like — the color physically detaches from the fiber and returns to solution. The question the test is asking: can this dye be stripped?

The three ingredients together recreate the two-key environment we established in Movement III §1. Forty-day fermented urine produces ammonia — raising the pH to 9–10 — and supports active alkaliphilic bacteria that provide the reducing environment. Plant indigo, which relies entirely on its hydrogen bond lattice for adhesion, cannot withstand this. The lattice dismantles, the color exits the fiber. A thread dyed with chilazon carries the bromine shield established in Movement III §3 — a structurally higher stripping threshold. It should survive.

Should — but not always. pH and bacterial activity are hard to control with precision. A bath that runs too aggressive can overwhelm even the bromine shield. A thread that survives Test 1 is definitively authentic. But a thread that fails is not definitively plant indigo. It may be genuine tekhelet that encountered an overly harsh calibration. Test 1 alone cannot close the question.

<Callout type="gold"><b>The forty-day specification is a calibration.</b> The original murex vat dyeing process likely used lye — sodium hydroxide, pH 13–14. The test deliberately steps down from those conditions. Forty-day fermented urine delivers a Goldilocks pH of roughly 9–10: aggressive enough to strip kala ilan, which fails at moderate alkalinity, but not strong enough to overcome the bromine shield of authentic tekhelet. Urea converts to ammonia through bacterial action and stabilizes at that equilibrium pH once fully consumed. During active fermentation the pH can spike unpredictably. Forty days guarantees complete conversion and a stable baseline.</Callout>

<Callout type="blue"><b>The roles of alum and fenugreek are uncertain.</b> Alum is mildly acidic in solution and would pull the bath pH downward, narrowing the calibration window further. Whether it is present to open the wool fiber for greater permeability, or to buffer and fine-tune the alkalinity, the Talmud does not say — and the two readings are not mutually exclusive. Fenugreek contains fermentable polysaccharides that could sustain bacterial populations through a forty-day fermentation, though we cannot confirm this is the intent.</Callout>

This is why there is a Test 2. And Test 2 probes something entirely different.

Sourdough is acidic. The baking environment delivers acid and heat together — conditions hostile to any ordinary dye, capable of degrading color and damaging the fabric itself. Under these conditions, plant indigo can only deteriorate. <span class="heb-inline">אִישְׁתַּנַּאי לִגְרִיעוּתָא</span>.

Authentic murex dye is a mixture of indigoid compounds — MBI, DBI, and indigotin in varying proportions, as we established in Movement III §3. Each compound has a characteristic thermochromic threshold. MBI reorganizes its molecular aggregates at a lower temperature; DBI requires more heat. During the dyeing process itself, heat may already have triggered the MBI shift — setting those aggregates permanently into their ordered form. But DBI, with its higher threshold, may not yet have been activated. It is still available to respond.

The sourdough baking environment brings DBI to its activation point. The aggregates reorganize. The color shifts toward a deeper, more saturated red-purple. This is <span class="heb-inline">אִישְׁתַּנַּאי לִמְעַלְּיוּתָא</span> — not merely survival under hostile conditions, but a positive change that reveals the molecular identity of what is in the fiber. Plant indigo has no DBI. It has no thermochromic mechanism at any temperature. It can only degrade.

The test does not read a specific color. It reads the direction of change. A color that improves under conditions designed to destroy it is making a chemical declaration: brominated indigoids are present, and their bromine is intact.

One question remains. Rashi describes the sourdough as <span class="heb-inline">חֲמִירָא אַרְכְּסָא</span> — thick, hardened. The thermochromic shift requires a hydrated environment. Does hardened baked sourdough provide sufficient moisture for DBI activation? The text does not describe immersion in liquid. If the sourdough is truly dry, the conditions may not be fully met. How the ancient dyers managed the moisture in this test remains an open question.

<Callout type="violet"><b>A speculative reading of the sourdough test.</b> The thermochromic shift in Ziderman's process is sensitive to the ratio of MBI, DBI, and indigotin in the starting material — a ratio that varies by snail, sex, age, and season. Ziderman's results reflect this: the color achieved is inherently variable, and knowing when to stop heating is a matter of practiced judgment. The dyer removes the wool when satisfied with the blue, which means the MBI may not yet have reached full stable aggregation. Leaving the wool in for longer, and thus raising the temperature further, risks the color shifting to a more violet hue.<br/>The sourdough environment may be doing more than providing acid. The dough's thermal mass creates a bounded heat environment — enough to complete the remaining MBI aggregation and improve the blue, but not enough to activate DBI and shift the color toward purple. In this reading the sourdough is a calibrated heat shield as much as an acid bath: it defines a temperature ceiling that separates the authentic improvement from the overshoot. The fact that purple would technically be a more saturated color — argaman rather than tekhelet — means that regardless of the outcome, true tekhelet would "improve" in color. It just may not be desirable for tzitzit.</Callout>
