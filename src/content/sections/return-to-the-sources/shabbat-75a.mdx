<p class="bt">In the previous movement we built the chemistry of tekhelet from the ground up — the chromophore, the hydrogen bond lattice, the two keys, the bromine shield, the three indigoid compounds. We did this without reference to the Talmudic sources. Now we return to the sources. What we will find is that the Sages, working without access to this chemistry, encoded a precision that the chemistry now explains. We begin with Shabbat 75a.</p>

<SubHead text="The sugya — Shabbat 75a" />

<p class="bt">The Gemara in Shabbat 75a takes up the Shabbat prohibitions involved in producing tekhelet. A Baraita is cited regarding one who traps the chilazon and pierces it. The discussion turns on which melachot apply — and why one of them, surprisingly, does not.</p>

<SubHead text="Commentary" />

<p class="bt">The Baraita establishes: trapping the chilazon and piercing it carries one Shabbat liability — צידה, trapping. Rabbi Yehuda says two: in his view, the piercing itself constitutes a form of דישה — the melacha of threshing, understood as the extraction of a substance from its natural casing. The Rabbis disagree: דישה does not apply here. Rava explains their reasoning: since the chilazon is not a plant, no דישה is involved, for דישה only applies for plants that grow on the ground.</p>

<p class="bt">The Gemara then raises a question: setting aside the question of דישה entirely — the piercing kills the animal. Why is there no additional liability for נטילת נשמה, the taking of life? Two answers are given.</p>

<p class="bt">Rabbi Yochanan resolves the problem by reframing the case: the snail was already dead when pierced — שפצעו מת. Where there is no living creature, there is no נטילת נשמה.</p>

<p class="bt">Rava answers that one is not liable for taking a life because that is not his intent at all. The intent is merely to extract the dye. The Gemara challenges this: since it is a certainty that the organism will die, one cannot claim lack of intent. Rava answers: <span class="heb-inline">כַּמָּה דְּאִית בֵּיהּ נְשָׁמָה טְפֵי נִיחָא לֵיהּ</span> — as long as it retains life, it is more beneficial to him. Rashi glosses: so that the appearance of its color should be <span class="heb-inline">צלולה</span> — clear, pure.</p>

<p class="bt">Here the chemistry we established in the previous movement becomes precise. The hypobranchial gland holds two components in cellular separation: the tyrindoxyl sulfate precursor and the enzyme purpurase that cleaves it. The piercing — הפוצעו — does both things at once: it releases the precursor fluid and ruptures the cellular compartments that kept purpurase isolated, introducing viable enzyme into the substrate. The cascade begins at that moment.</p>

<p class="bt">Purpurase denatures within seconds of the snail's death. A post-mortem piercing still releases the precursor — but the enzyme is no longer fully viable. The tyrindoxyl sulfate cannot be fully cleaved. What remains uncleaved becomes an insoluble, colorless contaminant in the final dye. The color is not merely less vivid. It is a race against time, and every second after death introduces chemically adulterated admixture of precursors that will never develop into dye.</p>

<p class="bt">This is what Rashi means by <span class="heb-inline">צלולה</span>. Clarity of color is the observable outcome of complete enzymatic conversion. Incomplete enzymatic conversion — the result of the organism's death — reduces conversion to dye and introduces impurities from the unconverted precursors. Death is contrary to the dyer's purpose because it is contrary to the chemistry.</p>

<p class="bt">Rava&#8217;s rule &#8212; that the chilazon alive at piercing yields a cleaner dye &#8212; is not a general truth about sea creatures. It is a chemically specific truth about Muricidae. It requires an enzyme/substrate separation architecture, purpurase denaturation at death, and consequent contamination from unprocessed precursors. No other proposed chilazon candidate has this architecture. Rava&#8217;s statement, read chemically, is positive evidence for murex identification &#8212; not merely consistent with it.</p>
