<p class="bt">Two tests. Both probe the same underlying reality from different angles. Test 1 establishes that authentic tekhelet resists the stripping conditions that dissolve ordinary vat dye. Test 2 establishes that authentic tekhelet responds to heat in a way ordinary dye cannot. Together they point to a single molecular property: brominated indigoids. The bromine shield accounts for the resistance. The thermochromic mechanism accounts for the response. To this writer, the tests are evidence that the Sages assumed authentic tekhelet carried both properties — that the dye they were authenticating was brominated.</p>

<p class="bt">The evidence points toward the murex as the source of tekhelet, and toward an ancient process that preserved the bromine rather than removing it. A process that debrominated the dye would produce a molecule the tests cannot confirm — not because such a dye is necessarily invalid, but because the tests were not designed with it in mind.</p>

<p class="bt">It is important to be precise about what this is and what it is not. This is a chemical reading of Talmudic evidence, not a halachic ruling. For halachic purposes, the question of validity turns on different considerations. It is likely that any dye-producing mollusk from the sea would be acceptable, and that any process yielding the desired color from such a mollusk would similarly be acceptable. The tests speak to what the authentic ancient product was. They do not close the halachic question of what today's product must be.</p>
