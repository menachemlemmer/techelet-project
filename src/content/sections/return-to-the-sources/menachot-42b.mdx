<p class="bt">Our stated aim in the introduction to Movement III was to faithfully reconstruct the ancient tekhelet dyeing method based on Talmudic sources. Menachot 42b records a direct exchange. Abaye asks Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yehuda how tekhelet was dyed. The answer: we bring the blood of the chilazon and sammanin, and cast them into the yora. The wool follows.</p>

<SubHead text="Commentary" />

<p class="bt">Rashi identifies sammanin as alum — צריף, called בייצ"א in Old French. The Talmudic text is brief. It does not describe what the yora is doing beyond receiving the ingredients and the wool.</p>

<p class="bt">In Movement III §5 we traced two paths to achieving the blue dye. The Talmudic description can map onto either path.</p>

<p class="bt">In the first reading, the dam hachilazon is fresh precursor fluid — tyrindoxyl already freed from sulfate by purpurase at the moment of piercing. It is still in soluble form. Alum is used to open the wool fiber. The dye enters directly. Color develops later upon exposure to air and light. No reduction is needed because the molecule has not yet oxidized. This seems to be Rashi's understanding.</p>

<p class="bt">In the second reading, the dye has already oxidized to brominated indigotin before entering the vat. To dissolve it and drive it into the fiber requires alkaline reduction. Sammanin in this reading is more than alum — it includes a strong alkali, perhaps lye. Pliny the Elder, writing in the first century, describes a twelve-day process: three days of salt-water soaking to develop bacterial activity, followed by nine days of continuous heating in lead vats. Salt alone is not sufficient for full reduction, and Pliny acknowledges his recipe is incomplete. But the duration and the bacterial window he describes are consistent with a reduction vat.</p>

<p class="bt">Which path the Talmud was referring to remains an open question. Regardless of the path taken, the tests we will examine in the next section remain the same.</p>
