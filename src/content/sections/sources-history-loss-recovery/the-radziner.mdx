For 1400 years the sacred and princely tekhelet blue disappeared from human practice. Continuous European oppression under the Church and the long exile of the Jewish people so far away from their native land on the Mediterranean coast all but erased its memory. Jewish practice settled on wearing the white fringes alone. And under the yoke of Christendom, Judaism adapted. Scholarship flourished in the blood-soaked lands of Ashkenaz. Spiritual revival movements periodically raised up the souls of downtrodden men and women and brought them closer to the light. Each revival saw itself as a progressive step toward the redemption. The most prominent of these in recent memory was Hasidism.

Deep in the Ukrainian heartland, where Orthodox priests, unwashed Cossacks, and poor Jews lived in their shtetls, the light of the Baal Shem Tov kindled flames of religious revival. Wonders and miracles, mystical devotion, and a fresh and more personal divine-human relationship emerged. The movement swept through the communities of Eastern Europe like a tidal wave. The Baal Shem Tov himself associated his movement with a messianic mission — but unlike the false messiahs that preceded him, it was not about the person or the moment. It was the movement itself, and the longer sweeping arc it would trace, that would contribute to the eventual redemption.

About a century and a half after the Baal Shem Tov, another Hasidic Rebbe — not one leading the larger sects — emerged as a central figure in our tekhelet story. His name was Rabbi Gershon Henoch Leiner of Radzyn. To understand this most interesting persona, we must first understand both his family history and legacy, and the time and place of his activity.

R' Menachem Mendel Morgenstern of Kotzk (1787–1859) was the premier Hasidic Rebbe of Poland. After R' Bunim of Peshischa died in 1827, his greatest disciples gathered under the leadership of the Kotzker. The Kotzker Rebbe was known for his singular focus on the pursuit of truth. False premises, ego narratives, ulterior motives — the Rebbe mercilessly dismantled them. His understanding of the deepest defenses and mechanisms of human nature are remarkable to this day. So many of his one-liners and deeper interpretations are still repeated and relevant in Hasidic discussions. His son-in-law was the halachic giant the Avnei Nezer of Sochachow (1838–1910), who speaks about his father-in-law's genius in uncommon terms. But this harsh criticism and self-awareness resulted in an environment of fear and terror. Eventually the Kotzker Rebbe himself retreated from the world — a recluse, alone in his room.

Among the leading disciples of the Kotzker was a dynamic young thinker who could not have been more different than his Rebbe. His name was R' Mordechai Yosef Leiner of Izbica, later known for his Hasidic work, the Mei Hashiloach. When Hasidim would come to Kotzk for the holidays, they would barely get a glimpse of the Rebbe — perhaps a harsh mumble that would crush them, perhaps not even that. But R' Mordechai Yosef was different. He would gather the Hasidim, take them on walks, and discuss his lofty and controversial ideas. For many Hasidim, the trip to Kotzk was more for the disciple than for the Rebbe.
