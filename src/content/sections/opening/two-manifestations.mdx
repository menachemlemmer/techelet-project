When the Torah opens the Ten Commandments with the words <span class="heb-inline">אָנֹכִי ה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ</span> — I am the Lord your God — Rashi asks why the verse needs to identify God as the one who took us out of Egypt. The answer he brings, citing the Mechilta, goes to the heart of what the people at Sinai had experienced.

<AramaicBlock segments={[
  {
    hebrew: "לְפִי שֶׁנִּגְלָה בַיָּם כְּגִבּוֹר עוֹשֶׂה מִלְחָמוֹת, וְנִגְלָה כָאן כְּזָקֵן מָלֵא רַחֲמִים — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְתַחַת רַגְלָיו כְּמַעֲשֵׂה לִבְנַת הַסַּפִּיר״ — זוֹ הָיְתָה לְפָנָיו בִּשְׁעַת הַשִּׁיעְבּוּד — ״וּכְעֶצֶם הַשָּׁמַיִם לָטֹהַר״ — מִשֶּׁנִּגְאֲלוּ. הוֹאִיל וַאֲנִי מִשְׁתַּנֶּה בַּמַּרְאוֹת, אַל תֹּאמְרוּ שְׁתֵּי רָשׁוּיוֹת הֵן — אָנֹכִי הוּא אֲשֶׁר הוֹצֵאתִיךָ מִמִּצְרַיִם וְעַל הַיָּם.",
    translation: "Because He revealed Himself at the sea as a warrior making war, and He revealed Himself here as an elder full of compassion — as it is said: \"And beneath His feet was like the work of a sapphire pavement\" — this was before Him at the time of the bondage — \"and like the very body of the sky in its purity\" — after they were redeemed. Since I appear differently in different visions, do not say there are two powers — I am the One who took you out of Egypt and at the sea."

  }
]} />
The people had witnessed two radically different manifestations of the divine. At the sea — a warrior, splitting waters, destroying an army. At Sinai — a sage, giving Torah, full of compassion. The first commandment anchors both to a single God: the same One who acted at the sea stands before you now at Sinai. The verse identifies itself precisely because the two experiences could seem to belong to two different powers.

But Rashi does something more precise than simply note the contrast. He reads the single verse from the Sinai vision — Shemot 24:10 — as encoding both experiences simultaneously. The two halves of one verse describe two different perceptions of the same divine presence. <span class="heb-inline">כְּמַעֲשֵׂה לִבְנַת הַסַּפִּיר</span> — like a brick of sapphire — this image was present before God at the time of the exodus. <span class="heb-inline">וּכְעֶצֶם הַשָּׁמַיִם לָטֹהַר</span> — like the very body of the sky in its purity — this image belongs to Sinai after redemption.

The essence did not change. What the observers perceived did.

Now a question presents itself. Yechezkel, in his vision of the divine chariot, describes the throne of glory in these terms:

<AramaicBlock segments={[
  {
    hebrew: "כְּמַרְאֵה אֶבֶן סַפִּיר דְּמוּת כִּסֵּא",
    translation: "Like the appearance of a sapphire stone — the likeness of a throne."

  }
]} />
Sapphire is the color of the throne of glory. It is the deepest rung of the chain — what tekhelet ultimately points toward. Yet Rashi assigns the sapphire image in the Sinai verse to the lower revelation: the warrior at the sea, the tangible miracle, the more external manifestation of divine power. And the sky image — less substantial, more diffuse — he assigns to the higher: the sage at Sinai, the giving of Torah, the interior spiritual encounter.

A sapphire that by rights belongs at the summit of the chain is placed at the base. A sky that is intermediate in the chain is placed at the height. Something in this assignment demands explanation — and the explanation will require us to look more carefully at what these colors actually are.
