<p class="bt">At Sinai, the Jewish people stood at the foot of the mountain and received the Torah as a direct encounter with the divine. The elders later ascended and saw beneath the feet of God something like a brick of Sapir, like the very body of the sky in its purity. A vision of color. A vision of light. With that vision came a responsibility — to live lives oriented toward the interior, toward the soul, toward the divine light that animates the visible world from within.</p>

<p class="bt">The color of that vision did not disappear with the moment. It was woven into the fabric of Jewish law. The Talmud draws a chain of resemblances from the blue thread of tzitzit to the sea, from the sea to the sky, from the sky to the throne of glory — the same throne glimpsed at Sinai in the color of Sapir. In this shiur we will ask what that chain means, and why it was constructed the way it was. Along the way we will examine the mitzvah of Tekhelet that is meant to remind us of these. We will use physics and chemistry as lenses that reveal what the texts themselves are pointing toward.</p>
