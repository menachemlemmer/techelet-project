### `light-and-color/the-sky.mdx`
**Claim:** "Blue light (~450nm) is scattered roughly ten times more efficiently than red light (~700nm)."
✅ Confirmed by Rayleigh's λ⁻⁴ law: (700/450)⁴ ≈ 9.4×. Confirmed as ~10x by multiple physics sources.
- **Primary source:** Rayleigh, Lord (J.W. Strutt). "On the light from the sky, its polarization and colour." *Philosophical Magazine* 41 (1871): 107–120, 274–279.
- **For general citation:** any standard atmospheric optics text; or Bohren, C.F. & Huffman, D.R. *Absorption and Scattering of Light by Small Particles*. Wiley, 1983.

**Claim:** "Named after Lord Rayleigh (1871)."
✅ Correct year. See Rayleigh 1871 above.

### `light-and-color/lapis-lazuli.mdx`
**Claim:** "Lapis lazuli mined primarily in Afghanistan."
✅ Well established. See Herrmann, G. "Lapis Lazuli: The Early Phases of Its Trade." *Iraq* 30(1) (1968): 21–57.

**Claim:** "Sapir almost certainly refers to lapis lazuli — true sapphire was unknown in the ancient Near East."
🔍 See M2 citations below for Theophrastus, Smith's Bible Dictionary, and Millard. This claim appears in both M1 and M2; the sources in M2 cover it.

**Claim:** Lazurite's color "comes from sulfur radical anions" with "peak absorption at 610–620nm."
✅ The chromophore is the trisulfur radical anion (S•⁻₃). Absorption range 595–620nm confirmed.
- Bikiaris, D. et al. "Sulfur K-edge XANES of lazurite: Toward determining the provenance of lapis lazuli." *Microchemical Journal* 125 (2016): 8–18. (ScienceDirect: doi:10.1016/j.microc.2015.10.032)
- Also: Wikipedia, "Lapis Lazuli" — cites multiple peer-reviewed spectroscopy studies for this specific claim.
- 📌 **Note:** The claim as stated says "peak absorption at 610–620nm." More precisely, the absorption band is 595–620nm with the peak around 610nm. This is accurate.
